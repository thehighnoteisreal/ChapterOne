High Notes Club

Eye Witness Accounts

Posted: February 23

I used a lot of words like “proof,” and “evidence” in my last post. Today, I’d like to share more details.

I first learned about the possible existence of this miracle website while I was working at a small Internet Service Provider in Howell, M in 1997I. One of our subscribers called to ask about an unusual email she had gotten in her inbox. I didn’t understand what was happening, but I’ve reconstructed the conversation using my notes, emails, and the recollections of my former coworkers.

Me: Argus ISP. How can I help you?

Jill: I’m calling to ask about an email message I recieved this morning.

Me: An email message?

Jill: Yes, it was very strange. And, I’ve only been using electronic mail for less than a month and haven’t shared it with very many people. I don’t understand how anybody would find out about it.

Me: Well, we don’t share our subscriber’s email addresses, Ma’am.

Jill: That’s good. Why did I get it, then?

Me: In what way was the email message strange?

Jill: There wasn’t a sender’s name, or an email address.

Me: Well, that can’t happen.

Jill: I swear that’s what I’m seeing.

I asked Jill to forward me the email message, and as it turns out she was right about there being no sender information attached. All of my co-workers felt like this could be done, but weren’t sure how it would have been managed or who would be able to pull it off.

The second thing which was weird about the message was that it addressed Jill by name, and was instructing her to send another message to another email address describing her biggest wish or problems. It seemed to imply that her email would be responded to. Jill hadn’t followed the instructions before forwarding it on to us, and I believe she deleted it by the next day.

After a few more days of discussion and some research, we dismissed it. Chain letter were real big back then, so we figured that it was someone’s new spin on an old idea.

In the early 2000’s, my friends and I were discussing the emergence of social networks. My friend, Bradley, asked the rest of us if we had gotten friend requests from a strange profile. When we all said that we hadn’t, he described a friend request which had appeared on his list of notifications without a name or a profile image. We all wrote it off as a glitch, until he told us what it had said.

The invitation had asked him to respond with a description of a problem he would like to have solved, or a life long wish. I was reminded of my conversation with Jill, and eventually realized that these two messages sounded very similar. It was almost as if the same person who had sent the email five years ago was now trying the same tactics on MySpace.
